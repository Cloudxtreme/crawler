Technologically speaking, Life    There are really two separate things to review on this one: the game, and the story.

    The game is a huge step forward for this genre. The world is beautiful, and well served by its indie soundtrack. The time travel mechanic is a great addition, because it allows you to try different choices on the spot. Again, just a huge step forward for this type of game. Technologically speaking, Life is Strange looks and plays like it's from a completely different decade than any of the other story games.

    The story is a bit more subjective. Just as billions of girls (and, in fairness, less combat oriented guys) around the world feel no instinctive kinship or investment when they're handed a controller and find themselves playing a floating AK-74 on a giant noisy battlefield, I'm sure some guys will need a moment to invest in a teenage girl whose life revolves around photography and trying to survive her final year of high school. While discovering that she can control time. There's a lot of shop talk on the photobug side of things. This game name drops famous photographers in the exact same way that the Treme writers were always trying to prove they knew more about jazz than their audience.

    Personally, I like the main character, Max, a great deal, and I enjoy her art school a lot more than I enjoy her time travel. We've all been a teenager in an unfamiliar new place. We can all remember the pressures of potential clashing with inertia as we approached the end of high school. The fact that she is a girl does not affect my ability to identify with Max. (You're a human, from Earth, you really should be able to fully empathize with all TWO of the genders on your homeworld, ffs.)

    I would, however, give this single warning to the less artsy people in the audience: If you could never enjoy being an art museum, large sections of this story will probably seem boring and stupid to you. The photography is not a line of background on a bio when you press C, it is who Max is, and what she does, and what she thinks about, much of the time. The complaint that this game is aimed at hipsters is far, far more valid than the ridiculous idea that the target demographic is teen girls, which is no more true of Life is Strange than it was of Buffy the Vampire Slayer.

    All in all, I would call Life is Strange a tremendous step forward, not just for story games, but for the general level of narrative in a medium that so often struggles to find a mature, adult voice.    … Expand

Every teen drama trope is there and its so heavy-handed that I found myself cringing my face off.

    Conversations feel forced and it's always very apparent that you're listening to something being read by a voice actor, rather than a believable, organic-feeling character. Nothing feels natural.

    Quality writing and acting makes you forget that what you're seeing and hearing isn't real, but everything about this game feels calculated to appeal to a very specific demographic of special snowflake types. As a result, Max is the -only- character so far who actually feels like she could be a real person, because everyone else feels so "written" and exists only for her, which is exactly how the type of narcissist who would enjoy this "game" perceives themselves and the world. Always weeping about how no one notices her despite being an unusually pretty girl, although to be fair, basically everyone in this game is pretty.

    Most of the "gameplay" is walking around listening to Max's internal monologue comment on anything and everyone you click on. There is a very clearly laid out, linear story going on here, which the game really doesn't want you to disrupt. And even when you do get to make a choice which actually matters, the game actually goes out of its way to remind you that the choice will have consequences and that you can rewind to change it.

    The whole thing feels like a teenager's power fantasy, better suited for a movie. Lots of films have already done this as a comedy, where LIS is basically the same concept presented as a pretentious, unintentionally funny art house non-game. The closest it comes to laughing at itself is when it "says things ironically," but much like real people who "say things ironically," it fails to convince you that this is actually the case.
