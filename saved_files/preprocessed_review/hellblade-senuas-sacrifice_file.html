Team Ninja’s design philosophy of making a “AAA Indie” game is an admirable one, and something I’d love to see them do again, with other developers following suit. The unique way in which the game was developed absolutely shows through when you experience it for yourself.

    Story-wise, it’s a sympathetic, interesting portrayal of mental illness on a scale that has never been attempted in a game before to my knowledge. It’s done really really well, and I feel like writing more about that, but I don’t want to spoil anything. The game is incredibly atmospheric, creepy and unsettling, but it’s not a horror game as such. It tentatively dips its feet into psychological horror the further you get in, as the atmosphere gets more ominous and the challenges more dangerous, but it avoids most of the horror tropes we're used to in games, and doesn't have any jump scares. The intention seems to be less on frightening the player and more on setting the mood and tone, and giving you an insight into Senua’s mind.

    Probably the most impressive thing this game has to offer is the way it uses sound. I simply can't remember a time when I played a game that was so focused on sound. The voices in Senua's (and your) head are important not just for the story, but also for the way that the game is played. Sometimes they encourage you, sometimes discourage you, sometimes they help and sometimes they don't. You'll need to listen as they give you clues (or lead you astray), and during combat they’ll do the same, warning you when an enemy is behind you, telling you to move and attack at the right moments... Or they may be more pessimistic, and simply whisper about your imminent death. You'll sometimes need to follow a sound during exploration, or listen carefully for the sound of an enemy you can’t see, but need to avoid. They obviously devoted a lot of time to the sound in this game, and it really shows. This game MUST be played with headphones, both for atmosphere and for gameplay purposes.

    As far as gameplay goes, I think this game could be most aptly described as an Action Puzzle game. Apart from a few maze puzzles, the rest of the puzzles are about matching shapes on the screen to the environment around you. Most of the time if you're observant and have a little patience, they aren't difficult to figure out. But occasionally they can become more ambiguous, and it can be annoying when you're *positive* that those two crooked trees create an "M' shape, but the game disagrees. It really would have benefited with some more traditional puzzle solving as well, just to mix things up and throw some more variety in there. Because unfortunately I got tired of this concept when I was around half-way through, and that's quite a big problem for a game with such a large focus on them.

    Sadly, I have mixed feelings about the combat too. The combat is a side-order to the puzzles, and exists primarily to break up the exploring and puzzle solving, and to keep the world feeling dangerous and engaging. That doesn't mean you can avoid it though, or breeze through it. Sometimes it can be intense, especially the boss fights - but when it doesn't go your way it can be infuriating. The reason for this is because although it's very "cinematic", it's not very dynamic. When you boil it down, it's fairly simplistic, stiff, and repetitive. The camera is firmly stuck on a low FOV directly behind Senua at all times (except when it clips through the environment and focuses on a f*cking wall), which creates an effective claustrophobic feel, but doesn't lend itself well to gameplay. You can only really focus on one enemy at a time, and your moves are quite limited. It feels pretty clunky, especially when you're surrounded. This was likely intentional, as a way to make the player feel vulnerable, but there are ways to do that without making them feel restricted... I can't deny that it fits the game’s style and is certainly engaging, but in the end it’s just not very enjoyable when you’re fighting more than 1 or 2 enemies. Similar to your limited moveset, the enemy variety is very limited too, which doesn’t help matters; because you really feel like you’re just going through the same motions over and over again.

    The atmosphere, the story, the art style and the sound are all totally on point. But both the puzzles and the combat leave something to be desired, and that's the whole "game" part of the game. So that’s unfortunate. I’d really love a sequel where they improved on these things, and despite my annoyances, I really hope this one sells well. You can tell it was made with care, with a lot of thought and a lot of heart, and though I do have mixed feelings, I'm glad I had this experience.

    A dark action adventure game that focuses primarily on storytelling. The graphics are bleeding edge compared to most other AAA games as of recent, which is a pleasant surprise.

    Lots of work was put into making sure the textures and models were clear and realistic enough that they could even be passed off as being taken straight out of a movie. The high production value is also not something you see everyday in a video game.

    STORY
    Hellblade talks about an exile who lost the man she loved and is having difficulty coping with that reality. Broken and suffering from psychosis, she embarks on a lonesome journey to "defy the darkness" in an effort to bring him back from death.

    Her visions get increasingly worse as she heads deeper into "Hel", defying the voices in her head who constantly tell her to give up on her love and to move on. In a way, the story plays out similarly to the stories of the first few God of War games.

    The story can be somewhat confusing at times due to the use of Norse mythology, but if you take the overall plot of Senua's suffering at face value, it becomes understandable. The goal of the game is to immerse the player into the world of demons that Senua travels through.

    GAMEPLAY
    This game is primarily an adventure game with added fighting scenes and puzzles. Puzzles are a primary factor of this game and they act as trials required to progress the story. Some of the puzzles are difficult while some are straightforward.

    Permanent death exists in this game if you die too much, but you are explicitly warned about it in the very beginning. If you fear the permadeath aspect, lower the difficulty in the settings. I personally haven't found it to be a big deal though, since the game is pretty generous with dying and I think each new area you reach "resets" your lives.

    The combat appears simplistic due to the limited actions available and the predictable enemies, but it doesn't mean you can blindly fight and expect to win. You most certainly cannot just "mash" the attack button. Not knowing when to dodge or block an attack can cost you a life. Don't expect it to be a walk in the park as the enemies usually gang up on you in groups and come at you in waves.

    Difficulty by default is set to "Auto". This means that if you die too much early on, the game will scale down its difficulty immediately. This is likely where all of the other reviewers got their notion of "easy" from: They've died too much. If you want to have the full experience, manually set the difficulty higher yourself.

    Hellblade is a largely linear game. Weapons and items don't exist in the game, nor are there any side quests or even people for that matter. You'll be doing a lot of walking and listening to the story unfold in her mind. The game does not have an "NG+" after it is over; you simply play through it once and then your save file is erased for you to start over, if you so desire.

    SUMMARY
    Not much can be said about this game without spoiling it. I recommend this game to anyone who wants to experience a unique story about psychosis. You won't regret spending the time to play this game, but don't set your expectations too high since the combat is not the main factor of the game.

    The game is somewhat short. You should be able to get around 7-10 hours out of the game, assuming you don't die too much or get stuck on the puzzles for too long.
