This might lead to some confusion as Borderlands was a first person shooter with some role play elements while Tales of the Borderlands is best described as an interactive story. Tales has therefore nothing in common with the Borderlands games but the setting and some cameos by characters.
    I wonder at what audience this game is actually targeted as it seems to me that the group of people who liked the Borderlands games are of a somewhat different disposition than those who like interactive stories, but maybe I am just mistaken.
    Tales is also not a game for those who like complex puzzles. Although Tales has some puzzles, these are far in between and not very complex and offer therefore no challenge.
    The game also sports dialog trees, but again this is of a rather superficial nature as the different choices do not play out in significant different ways. This might disappoint some people no doubt, especially when you are used to be able to make a significant impact on the story line.

    Tales is the story of Rhys and Fiona. Rhys is an employee of the malignant corporation Hyperion who tries to get back at his boss Vasquez by buying the Vault key Vasquez is trying to get his hands on before he is able to do so. Fiona is a con artist who tries to sell a fake Vault key to Hyperion as part of a con job that she and her sister Sasha try to pull of, helped by their replacement daddy and mentor Felix and the unknowing August, a small time gangster who is in love with Sasha.
    The whole deal gets messy fast when the vault key is found out to be a fake and at that same precise moment a gangster boss, Bossonova, chased by a vault hunter named Zer0, enters the scene and steals the ten million Rhys has 'borrowed' from Hyperion to buy the key. And now the ten million becomes the MacGuffin of the story that is filled with chases, lunatics, funny dialog, double crossing, odd characters, back stabbing, silliness and hilarious over the top scenes. But also with love, loyalty and friendship.
    Tales can best seen as a story with some interactive elements and should not be mistaken for a game. While you will be able to have some interaction with the story line, this will mostly be superficial.
    For me this might diminish the game somewhat, but because of the sheer fun it gives this can be forgiven.

    There are some dark linings though.
    At the moment the story is full of unfinished business and more loose ends are in the make in episode three as far as I can tell. The danger is that there are just too many of them to wrap it up neatly later on. The concept kill your darlings applies here as well. But there are still a few more episodes in the make, so let's give Tales the benefit of the doubt. An eight at least.    … Expand

The player is limited to a very small number of actions at all times, and apart from missing a quick-time event that leads to an instant death, never feels like actually being part of the story but simply a by-watcher.
